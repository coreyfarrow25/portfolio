# Tellegacy: Launching a Loneliness-Reduction Program at 25 Nursing Homes

> **PM Internship** · Jan 2026–Present · Program launch, grant strategy, operational infrastructure
>
> *Note: This case study covers program management work, not software product management. The PM-transferable skills — business case writing, stakeholder research, system design, launch planning — are the point.*

---

## The Problem

North Dakota nursing home residents experience high rates of loneliness and social isolation. Existing facility activities programs are inconsistent, under-resourced, and rarely structured around evidence-informed behavioral health outcomes. The cost — to resident quality of life and to facilities' regulatory standing — is real and measurable.

Tellegacy had a working model: trained student "Legacy Builders" conduct 10 structured one-on-one sessions with a nursing home resident, guiding them through reminiscence, goal-setting, and life-story work, culminating in a professionally bound Legacy Book. Prior pilots showed improvements in loneliness scores, mood, and engagement. The problem: the model existed at small scale with no funding to bring it to 25 North Dakota facilities simultaneously.

**Scaling this program required three things that didn't exist yet:**

1. A funded business case — a federal grant application that could secure $187,500 to run the program across 25 facilities for 12 months
2. A facility recruitment pipeline — a stakeholder research system to identify and contact the right people at ND nursing homes, healthcare associations, and university partners
3. A replicable program infrastructure — documentation and training frameworks that would allow facilities to operate independently after the grant period ended

All three became my scope.

---

## What I Owned

### 1. Federal Grant Application — CMP Business Case

The Civil Money Penalty (CMP) grant program redirects regulatory penalty funds from skilled nursing facilities back into resident-benefit initiatives. Writing the CMP application meant writing a product business case: problem statement, solution design, implementation timeline, stakeholder plan, success metrics, and budget justification — all under federal formatting requirements.

**My contributions to the application:**

- Drafted the program narrative: problem framing (ND nursing home loneliness rates), goals and objectives, 12-month implementation plan (Months 1–2: facility recruitment and setup; Months 3–11: weekly session delivery; Months 6–12: Legacy Book production; Month 12: evaluation and reporting)
- Built the budget justification framework: audited the draft budget spreadsheet for internal consistency, benchmarked comparable grant budgets from similar programs to standardize line-item justification language, and ensured every cost line traced to a specific program activity
- Documented the mileage calculation explicitly (9,880 miles × $0.725/mile = $7,113.60) rather than using a round number — a common red flag that funders associate with fabricated estimates
- Drafted the sustainability appendix: how facilities continue Tellegacy after CMP funding ends (train-the-trainer model, costs limited to staff time and printing, integration with existing QAPI tracking)
- Wrote the stakeholder involvement plan: how residents, families, and direct-care staff shape session timing, outreach messaging, and outcome reporting

**Budget scope:** $187,500 total ($7,500 per facility × 25 facilities). Program reach: up to 200 residents served (3–8 per facility × 25 facilities × 10 sessions each).

### 2. Grant Intelligence Database

To identify expansion funding beyond the CMP grant, I built a structured grant inventory tracking 40+ federal and foundation programs. This wasn't a list of links — it was a decision-support tool.

Each entry included: funder name, grant type, geographic eligibility, focus tags (loneliness, dementia care, intergenerational programs, nursing home quality), nursing home fit rating (High/Medium/Low), eligible applicant types, typical award size, duration, deadline date, deadline status, and a strategy note explaining why Tellegacy was or wasn't a good fit.

Programs tracked included NIA IMPACT Collaboratory pilot grants (up to $300K), NIH R01/R21 social isolation tracks, NEXT STEPs Network nursing home pilots, and multiple HRSA workforce and care quality programs.

**The design decision:** Using a structured tagging system rather than a freeform notes document meant the inventory could be filtered by fit rating, geography, and deadline status — useful for a small team managing multiple concurrent grant submissions with limited bandwidth.

### 3. Facility Recruitment Pipeline

Getting 25 nursing homes to agree to participate required identifying and contacting the right stakeholders at each level: facility administrators, state healthcare associations, university GWEP program partners, and ND HHS contacts.

I built the outreach contact database: researching the North Dakota Hospital Association, Long Term Care Association, ND Nurses Association, and 15+ professional organization contacts, identifying the right individual at each (director, coordinator, or program lead) rather than defaulting to generic email addresses. Designed the outreach research process for GWEP (Geriatric Workforce Enhancement Program) grantees — systematically identifying the best point of contact at each university partner.

**The judgment call on letters of participation:** Early in the process I flagged that letters of participation from facilities were the critical dependency — not the budget, not the narrative. A well-written grant with no facility commitments wouldn't pass review. This meant facility outreach needed to start before the application was finalized, not after.

## Key Decisions & Tradeoffs

**Train-the-trainer vs. Tellegacy-staffed delivery.** One design option was for Tellegacy staff to run all 25 facilities directly. I pushed for train-the-trainer because it changes the sustainability math: if Tellegacy has to be present to run sessions, the program collapses when funding ends. If facilities have trained staff champions, the program can continue at near-zero marginal cost. The CMP application sustainability section is built around this argument.

**Standardized justification language vs. facility-specific narratives.** Grant reviewers flag inconsistent formatting and vague cost bases as signs of inexperience. I chose to benchmark comparable approved budgets and apply consistent structure across all line items (purpose → basis of estimate → calculation → notes) — even where some items required more work to document — because a coherent budget signals organizational credibility.

**Grant inventory depth vs. breadth.** Early versions of the grant inventory had too many entries with too little detail (just a name and a link). I restructured it to track fewer programs with full fields completed, including fit assessment and a strategy note, so it was actually useful for grant writing prioritization rather than just a backlog.

## Outcome

- $187,500 CMP grant application drafted and submitted for review by ND HHS
- Grant intelligence database: 40+ programs tracked with deadline status, fit scoring, and strategy notes — designed to reduce administrative lead time for future grant applications by ~20% through standardized workflows
- Stakeholder contact database: 15+ ND healthcare associations and GWEP program contacts researched and documented for facility recruitment outreach
- Sustainability infrastructure: train-the-trainer model documented and included in grant appendix; program designed for near-zero cost continuation after CMP funding period

**The PM parallel:** Every component of this work maps to a product launch: the grant application is the business case, the grant inventory is the competitive/funding landscape analysis, the facility outreach is GTM, and the sustainability appendix is the post-launch ownership plan. The "product" is a human-centered intervention designed around specific user needs (resident loneliness, facility capacity constraints, volunteer training requirements). Scoping it well required the same skills as scoping any other product.
